\section{Experiments and Evaluations}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate \ssppp{} against five baselines on three questions: (1)~does the
bounded head cost predictive accuracy? (2)~does \ssppp{} reproduce the
structure of real order flow in closed-loop simulation? (3)~can each model be
rate-calibrated at all? We close with computational efficiency.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
\paragraph{Datasets.}
We benchmark on Coinbase BTC, ETH, and SOL event-driven order flow, seven days
per asset, encoded by the event constructor into $|\mathcal{E}|=62$ atomic
types ($20+20+2+20$ over LO/CO/MO/IS, sides, and levels). Validation-split
rates are $38.3$, $47.9$, and $24.0$ events/s: production event rates, not
subsampled streams.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
All models share the same input embedding and training configuration. They
differ only in the decoder.
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
\item \textbf{LSTM}: a standard LSTM decoder.

\item \textbf{\nhp{}} \citep{Mei2017TheProcess}: the neural Hawkes
  continuous-time LSTM.
\item \textbf{SAHP} \citep{zhang2020self}: a causal-attention decoder (adapted
  generic backbone).
\item \textbf{PCT-LSTM}: a per-type parallel continuous-time LSTM.
\item \textbf{\sppp{}} \citep{chang2025ctssm}: the unbounded state-space
  parent; identical backbone to \ssppp{}, unbounded softplus head.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Training and evaluation protocol.}
Each model trains per asset with sequence length 1024, non-overlapping
windows, identical gradient-step counts, and three training seeds. The fixed
1024-event context spans only ${\sim}20$--$40$\,s of market time at these
rates, so long-memory structure leans entirely on carried state. Evaluation is
strict end-to-end:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
\item \textbf{Prediction} is scored on \emph{every} genuine test event by a
  lossless streaming evaluator with carried state (one cold start per genuine
  segment boundary); the evaluator hard-fails if any event goes unscored.
\item \textbf{Simulation} uses carried-state closed-loop roll-outs
  ($32\times600$\,s, three roll-out seeds per checkpoint) against
  equal-duration bootstrap samples of real segments. \ssppp{} samples by Ogata
  thinning with its exact closed-form ceiling. Baselines, which admit no exact
  bound, share a common compensator-inversion sampler.
\item \textbf{Calibration}: every model is rate-calibrated before scoring by a
  one-dimensional bisection on the time-rescaling constant $\kappa$, targeting the
  \emph{validation}-split rate. A full-scale validation roll-out must then
  verify the target within 15\%. Checkpoints that fail are excluded and the
  failure is reported as a result. SAHP is the exception: its closed-loop rate
  is unstable across warm-starts and no constant $\kappa$ transfers, so it is
  reported uncalibrated ($\kappa{=}1$, marked \dag).
\item \textbf{Statistics}: roll-out seeds are averaged within each checkpoint.
  95\% CIs are $t$-based across checkpoints. Any batch failure or non-finite
  value aborts the run.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Prediction Quality}
\label{sec:prediction}
According to Table~\ref{tab:multiasset}, \ssppp{} is the strongest predictor
on two of the three assets: it wins \emph{every} prediction metric on SOL by a
wide margin ($0.27$ vs.\ $0.53$ overall NLL; $0.192$ vs.\ $0.184$ accuracy),
wins overall NLL and accuracy outright on ETH ($-0.87$ vs.\ \nhp's $-0.81$;
$0.345$ vs.\ $0.341$), and ties \nhp{} on BTC marks while conceding overall
likelihood. It achieves this while remaining verifiably calibratable in
closed loop (Table~\ref{tab:caloutcomes}). No baseline combines both.

The remaining gap is in timing, and we can locate it precisely. A per-gap
decomposition of timeNLL $= -\log\lambda(t_i) + \Delta u_i$ (event term $+$
compensator mass) on BTC, the asset where \nhp{} leads, shows that burst
sharpness is \emph{not} the issue: the state-space family's event terms beat
\nhp's ($-4.95$ \ssppp{} vs.\ $-4.23$), and intensity-ceiling saturation is
comparable across models (${\sim}25\%$). The entire gap is compensator mass in
\emph{quiet gaps}: mean per-gap rescaled mass is $1.88$ (\ssppp) and $2.26$
(\sppp) against \nhp's near-ideal $1.03$ (target $1$). The excess accrues in
gaps beyond $0.1$\,s, which are rare at $38$\,ev/s but expensive
(\ssppp{} integrates $10.0$ units of hazard across a $0.5$--$2$\,s gap where
\nhp{} integrates $6.0$; \sppp{} $16.4$). Figure~\ref{fig:hazard} shows why:
\nhp's per-dimension decay toward a \emph{learned steady state} tracks the
falling empirical hazard of real gaps, while the state-space family, whose
quiet-state behaviour is an emergent readout of decaying latents with no
dedicated resting-rate parameter, stays hot through quiet gaps. This cost
is the deliberate price of stability. What it buys is the subject of the next
two subsections.

\subsection{Simulation Quality}
\label{sec:simulation}
Table~\ref{tab:multiassetsf} and Figure~\ref{fig:forest} score calibrated
closed-loop roll-outs on the stylized facts of real order flow. Every
calibrated model lands its rate (rel-err $0.06$--$0.42$). The discriminating
columns are structural, and there \ssppp{} is the only model that pairs
competitive simulation with top-tier prediction: it is at or near the front on
every structural column of every asset, and it leads clustering on SOL.
Among the baselines, PCT-LSTM leads the Fano column but sits mid-table in
prediction; \nhp, the best BTC predictor, is mid-pack in simulation on every
asset; and the LSTM pins Fano rel-err at ${\approx}0.99$ throughout, because it
simulates a near-Poisson stream, the trivially-stable end of the trade-off.
Prediction and simulation quality are distinct axes, and no unbounded model
wins both.

Figure~\ref{fig:fano} shows the level-and-rise view on BTC: real Fano rises
from ${\sim}60$ at 1\,s to ${\sim}1000$ at 50\,s. Every model under-disperses
at this context length, \ssppp{} and PCT-LSTM least. The one surviving
\sppp{} arm on ETH calibrates in rate but over-disperses wildly (Fano rel-err
$6.0$): rate calibration cannot repair a near-critical checkpoint's structure.

\subsection{Calibratability}
\label{sec:calibratability}
Post-hoc rate calibration (Methods) applies to \emph{every} decoder, because a
constant $\kappa$ rescales sim-time intensity without touching type
probabilities, so we calibrate every model and treat the \emph{outcome} as
an experimental result. The protocol is deliberately falsifiable: probes must
bracket and converge to the validation target, a full-scale roll-out must
verify it, and every failure is reproducible (probe seeds are deterministic;
each retried failure reproduced its bisection bracket bit-identically).

Table~\ref{tab:caloutcomes} is, to our knowledge, the first cross-asset
quantification of closed-loop calibratability for neural TPPs: the bounded
\ssppp{} head verifies on \emph{all nine} checkpoints, while the unbounded
\sppp{} head fails on 7 of 9, and on SOL the model class is excluded
outright. Figure~\ref{fig:ladder} shows the failure signature. The closed-loop
rate of a failing \sppp{} checkpoint is near-discontinuous in $\kappa$: a
$10^{-3}$ change flips the realized rate across the entire tolerance band
(e.g.\ $36\to48.5$\,ev/s), and seed-to-seed variance at fixed $\kappa$ reaches
${\sim}30\%$. This is the behaviour of a system parked near criticality, where
no constant time-rescaling exists. The \ssppp{} response crosses the same
target smoothly. (An earlier run of one BTC seed produced a qualitatively
different, single failure: a \emph{metastable} burst-locked regime at
${\sim}2\times$ the target rate, bounded and benign next to \sppp's
criticality; retraining escaped that basin.) This confirms the stability
certificate empirically: the same unbounded softplus head whose
intensity-collapse states produce \sppp's pathological expected-time tail
(Table~\ref{tab:multiasset}) also places checkpoints near criticality in
closed loop, and both problems disappear under the softmin bound.

\subsection{Efficiency Analysis}
\label{sec:efficiency}
The state-space backbone is also computationally advantageous once its
per-event sequential loop is replaced by an exact associative prefix scan
(Hillis--Steele over the ZOH recurrence; identical states and gradients to
$2\!\times\!10^{-4}$). Table~\ref{tab:speed} reports wall-clock measurements
on one GPU (ETH checkpoints, benchmark configuration). The scan collapses the
\sppp-family training step from $3.5$\,s to ${\sim}0.06$\,s: attention-class
speed, $60\times$ faster than the loop and $73\times$ faster than the
CT-LSTM, turning a multi-hour training run into minutes. For sampling,
\ssppp's exact ceiling makes Ogata thinning available with a \emph{constant}
dominating rate (no per-step bound estimation; mean acceptance $0.54$ on
ETH): exactness costs ${\sim}2.5\times$ throughput against the approximate
inversion sampler shared by all models, and both run far above real time
($353$ vs.\ $48$ events/s on the fastest asset).
