\section{Experiments and Evaluations}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate \ssppp{} against five baselines on three questions: (1)~does the
bounded head cost predictive accuracy? (2)~does \ssppp{} reproduce the
structure of real order flow in closed-loop simulation? (3)~can each model be
rate-calibrated at all? We close with computational efficiency.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
\paragraph{Datasets.}
We benchmark on Coinbase BTC, ETH, and SOL event-driven order flow, seven days
per asset, encoded by the event constructor into $|\mathcal{E}|=62$ atomic
types ($20+20+2+20$ over LO/CO/MO/IS, sides, and levels). Validation-split
rates are $38.3$, $47.9$, and $24.0$ events/s: production event rates, not
subsampled streams.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
All models share the same input embedding and training configuration. They
differ only in the decoder.
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
\item \textbf{LSTM}: a standard LSTM decoder.

\item \textbf{\nhp{}} \citep{Mei2017TheProcess}: the neural Hawkes
  continuous-time LSTM.
\item \textbf{SAHP} \citep{zhang2020self}: a causal-attention decoder (adapted
  generic backbone).
\item \textbf{PCT-LSTM} \citep{Shi2022StateSimulation}: the state-dependent
  parallel neural Hawkes process (CT4LSTM-PPP), ported faithfully; its
  market-state input is disabled because the closed-loop protocol provides no
  exogenous state stream.
\item \textbf{\sppp{}} \citep{chang2025ctssm}: the unbounded state-space
  parent; identical backbone to \ssppp{}, unbounded softplus head.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Training and evaluation protocol.}
Each model trains per asset with sequence length 1024, non-overlapping
windows, identical gradient-step counts, and three training seeds. The fixed
1024-event context spans only ${\sim}20$--$40$\,s of market time at these
rates, so long-memory structure leans entirely on carried state. Evaluation is
strict end-to-end:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
\item \textbf{Prediction} is scored on \emph{every} genuine test event by a
  lossless streaming evaluator with carried state (one cold start per genuine
  segment boundary); the evaluator hard-fails if any event goes unscored.
\item \textbf{Simulation} uses carried-state closed-loop roll-outs
  ($32\times600$\,s, three roll-out seeds per checkpoint) against
  equal-duration bootstrap samples of real segments. \ssppp{} samples by Ogata
  thinning with its exact closed-form ceiling. Baselines, which admit no exact
  bound, share a common compensator-inversion sampler.
\item \textbf{Calibration}: every model is rate-calibrated before scoring by a
  one-dimensional bisection on the time-rescaling constant $\kappa$, targeting the
  \emph{validation}-split rate. A full-scale validation roll-out must then
  verify the target within 15\%. Checkpoints that fail are excluded and the
  failure is reported as a result. SAHP is the exception: its closed-loop rate
  is unstable across warm-starts and no constant $\kappa$ transfers, so it is
  reported uncalibrated ($\kappa{=}1$, marked \dag).
\item \textbf{Statistics}: roll-out seeds are averaged within each checkpoint.
  95\% CIs are $t$-based across checkpoints. Any batch failure or non-finite
  value aborts the run.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Prediction Quality}
\label{sec:prediction}
\textbf{Finding: bounding the intensity does not cost predictive accuracy ---
\ssppp{} is the strongest predictor on two of the three assets.} According to
Table~\ref{tab:multiasset}, \ssppp{} wins every prediction metric on SOL by a
wide margin ($0.27$ vs.\ the strongest baseline's $0.38$ overall NLL; $0.192$
vs.\ $0.184$ accuracy), wins overall NLL and accuracy outright on ETH ($-0.87$
vs.\ PCT-LSTM's $-0.84$; $0.345$ vs.\ \nhp's $0.341$), and on BTC ties
\nhp{} on marks ($0.448$ vs.\ $0.449$) while conceding overall likelihood.
It achieves this while remaining verifiably calibratable in closed loop
(Table~\ref{tab:caloutcomes}); no baseline combines both.

The remaining gap is in timing, and a per-gap decomposition of timeNLL
$= -\log\lambda(t_i) + \Delta u_i$ (event term $+$ compensator mass) locates
it precisely on BTC, the asset where \nhp{} leads. Burst sharpness is
\emph{not} the issue: the state-space family's event terms beat \nhp's
($-4.87$ \ssppp{} vs.\ $-4.29$), and intensity-ceiling saturation is
comparable across models (${\sim}25\%$). The entire gap is compensator mass
in \emph{quiet gaps}: mean per-gap rescaled mass is $1.85$ (\ssppp) and
$2.38$ (\sppp) against \nhp's near-ideal $1.07$ (target $1$). The excess
accrues in gaps beyond $0.1$\,s, which are rare at $38$\,ev/s but expensive:
\ssppp{} integrates $4.3$ units of intensity across a $0.5$--$2$\,s gap where
\nhp{} integrates $0.8$ (\sppp{} $3.0$).

The insight, visible in Figure~\ref{fig:hazard} on all three assets, is
mechanistic. \nhp's per-dimension decay toward a \emph{learned steady state}
tracks the falling empirical hazard of real gaps, while the state-space
family, whose quiet-state behaviour is an emergent readout of decaying latents
with no dedicated resting-rate parameter, stays hot through quiet gaps. On SOL
the unbounded \sppp{} additionally shows the opposite failure at long gaps:
its intensity collapses below the hazard, the same intensity-collapse
pathology behind its pathological expected-time errors. This quiet-gap cost is
the deliberate price of stability; what it buys is the subject of the next two
subsections.

\subsection{Simulation Quality}
\label{sec:simulation}
\textbf{Finding: \ssppp{} is the only model that pairs competitive
closed-loop simulation with top-tier prediction.} According to
Table~\ref{tab:multiassetsf} and Figure~\ref{fig:forest}, every calibrated
model lands its target rate (rel-err $0.09$--$0.42$), so the discriminating
columns are structural, and there \ssppp{} is at or near the front on every
column of every asset and leads both the Fano and clustering columns on SOL.

Among the baselines, each strength comes with a weakness elsewhere. PCT-LSTM
leads the Fano column on BTC and ETH but has the weakest type accuracy in
Table~\ref{tab:multiasset}; \nhp, the best BTC predictor, is mid-pack in
simulation on every asset; the LSTM pins Fano rel-err at ${\approx}0.99$
throughout because it simulates a near-Poisson stream, the trivially-stable
end of the trade-off; and the one surviving \sppp{} arm on ETH calibrates in
rate but over-disperses wildly (Fano rel-err $6.0$): rate calibration cannot
repair a near-critical checkpoint's structure. Figure~\ref{fig:fano} shows
the level-and-rise view: real Fano rises from ${\sim}70$ at 1\,s to
${\sim}1300$ at 50\,s on BTC, and the same pattern repeats on ETH and SOL.
Every model under-disperses at this context length, \ssppp{} and PCT-LSTM
least.

The insight is that prediction and simulation quality are distinct axes: no
unbounded model wins both, and the two families that simulate burst structure
best do it through opposite designs (per-type parallel streams vs.\ a bounded
shared rate), of which only the bounded design also predicts at the top of the
table.

\subsection{Calibratability}
\label{sec:calibratability}
Post-hoc rate calibration (Methods) applies to \emph{every} decoder, because a
constant $\kappa$ rescales sim-time intensity without touching type
probabilities, so we calibrate every model and treat the \emph{outcome} as
an experimental result. The protocol is deliberately falsifiable: probes must
bracket and converge to the validation target, a full-scale roll-out must
verify it, and every failure is reproducible (probe seeds are deterministic;
each retried failure reproduced its bisection bracket bit-identically).

\textbf{Finding: whether a model can be calibrated at all is a measurable
property, and the bound decides it.} Table~\ref{tab:caloutcomes} is, to our
knowledge, the first cross-asset quantification of closed-loop calibratability
for neural TPPs: the bounded \ssppp{} head verifies on \emph{all nine}
checkpoints, while the unbounded \sppp{} head fails on 7 of 9, and on SOL the
model class is excluded outright.

Figure~\ref{fig:ladder} shows the failure in detail. The closed-loop rate of
a failing \sppp{} checkpoint is near-discontinuous in $\kappa$: an
$8\times10^{-4}$ change flips the realized rate across the entire tolerance
band ($34.8\to42.3$\,ev/s), and repeated probes at fixed $\kappa$ differ by
${\sim}20\%$. This is the behaviour of a system parked near criticality,
where no constant time-rescaling exists. The \ssppp{} response crosses the
same target smoothly on every asset. (An earlier training run of one BTC seed
produced a checkpoint whose single failure was qualitatively different: a
per-lane diagnostic traced it to a \emph{metastable} burst-locked regime at
${\sim}2\times$ the target rate that individual roll-out lanes enter
stochastically, bounded and benign next to \sppp's criticality; retraining
escaped that basin.)

The insight closes the loop on the certificate: the same unbounded softplus
head whose intensity-collapse states produce \sppp's pathological
expected-time tail (Table~\ref{tab:multiasset}) also places checkpoints near
criticality in closed loop, and both problems disappear under the softmin
bound.

\subsection{Efficiency Analysis}
\label{sec:efficiency}
The state-space backbone is also computationally advantageous once its
per-event sequential loop is replaced by an exact associative prefix scan
(Hillis--Steele over the ZOH recurrence; identical states and gradients to
$2\!\times\!10^{-4}$). Table~\ref{tab:speed} reports wall-clock measurements
on one GPU (ETH checkpoints, benchmark configuration). The scan collapses the
\sppp-family training step from $3.5$\,s to ${\sim}0.06$\,s: attention-class
speed, $60\times$ faster than the loop and $73\times$ faster than the
CT-LSTM, turning a multi-hour training run into minutes. For sampling,
\ssppp's exact ceiling makes Ogata thinning available with a \emph{constant}
dominating rate (no per-step bound estimation; mean acceptance $0.54$ on
ETH): exactness costs ${\sim}2.5\times$ throughput against the approximate
inversion sampler shared by all models, and both run far above real time
($353$ vs.\ $48$ events/s on the fastest asset).
