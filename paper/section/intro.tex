\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The limit order book (LOB) is the core mechanism of electronic markets: a
continuous-time stream of order placements, cancellations, and trades whose
fine structure drives price discovery and liquidity. Two tasks are asked of a
generative model of this stream. The first is \emph{prediction}: given the
event history, score the time and type of the next event. The second is
\emph{simulation}: roll the model forward freely, feeding its own samples back
as history, and reproduce the stylized facts of real order flow, such as
calibrated event rates, burst--lull clustering, and heavy-tailed returns
\citep{cont2001empirical}. Simulation is the harder task and, for downstream
use (market-making world models, agent training, counterfactual analysis), the
more valuable one \citep{mars2025,nagy2025lobbench}.

Neural MTPPs \citep{du2016recurrent,Mei2017TheProcess,Shchur2021NeuralReview}
excel at prediction, and the continuous-time state-space model \sppp{}
\citep{chang2025ctssm,Shi2022StateSimulation} is the current state of the art:
it holds the best held-out likelihood among neural TPPs. It is therefore the
natural candidate for an LOB simulator. But its predictive power has only ever
been demonstrated teacher-forced. The \sppp{} paper never rolls the model out,
and we find that when it is rolled out, the state-of-the-art predictor is not
a usable simulator. Two obstacles are responsible. \emph{First, instability}:
the self-excitation that wins the one-step likelihood becomes a liability in
closed loop, because each sampled event raises the intensity, which produces
more events, and with no mechanism bounding the rate the roll-out drifts hot
or explodes. \emph{Second, uncalibratability}: the standard patch, rescaling
the intensity until the simulated rate matches reality, turns out to be
unavailable exactly where it is needed most. We find trained \sppp{}
checkpoints whose closed-loop rate is \emph{near-discontinuous} in the
rescaling constant, so that no constant rescaling exists. On one of our three
assets, every \sppp{} checkpoint fails, leaving no usable simulator at all.
The gap, then, is concrete: the model with the best prediction power cannot
simulate stably, while simple stable models simulate safely but predict
poorly.

\ssppp{} changes only the output heads of \sppp{}. The stacked
latent-linear-Hawkes state-space backbone is kept verbatim, with its diagonal
zero-order-hold dynamics, parallel-scan computability, and LayerNorm'd stack
output $h_t$. Everything after $h_t$ is replaced. Because the backbone is
identical, differences against the unbounded parent are attributable to the
heads. Our contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The \ssppp{} head pair}: a softmin-bounded rate head, whose
  intensity has a hard closed-form ceiling (an exact dominating rate for
  Ogata thinning) and an exactly-zero floor with unit log-intensity gradient
  in quiet periods, times a rate-neutral soft-max mark head that cannot
  change the total rate however deep its network. Together they turn \sppp{}
  into a provably stable simulator.
\item \textbf{Stable training and simulation.} Stateful (TBPTT) training
  walks contiguous stretches of the stream so the model never trains in a
  cold-start transient, and closed-loop roll-outs carry the packed decoder
  state at $O(1)$ cost per event, mathematically identical to encoding the
  full history and free of the sliding window's memory ceiling. Training and
  simulation see the same regime.
\item \textbf{Verified, exact post-hoc rate calibration.} For the factorized
  head the rate is linear in a learnable scale, the ceiling scales with it,
  and marks are untouched, so calibration is a one-dimensional root-find that
  keeps the guarantee intact. A mark-preserving variant applies to every
  baseline, with full-scale verification. Under this test, calibratability
  becomes a measurable model property: the unbounded parent frequently fails
  with a critical-point signature while the bounded head always verifies.
\end{enumerate}
