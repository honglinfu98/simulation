\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The limit order book (LOB) is the core mechanism of electronic markets: a
continuous-time stream of order placements, cancellations, and trades whose fine
structure drives price discovery and liquidity. Two tasks are asked of a generative
model of this stream. The first is \emph{prediction}: given the event history, score
the time and type of the next event. The second is \emph{simulation}: roll the model
forward freely, feeding its own samples back as history, and reproduce the stylized
facts of real order flow --- calibrated event rates, burst--lull clustering, and
heavy-tailed returns \citep{cont2001empirical}. Simulation is the harder and, for
downstream use (market-making world models, agent training, counterfactual analysis),
the more valuable of the two.

Neural MTPPs \citep{du2016recurrent,Mei2017TheProcess,Shchur2021NeuralReview} excel at
prediction, and state-space variants specialized to LOB streams
\citep{Shi2022StateSimulation} push accuracy further. But the very self-excitation
that wins the one-step likelihood becomes a liability closed-loop: each sampled event
raises the intensity, which produces more events, and with no mechanism bounding the
rate the roll-out runs away. In our benchmark the best predictor --- the Neural Hawkes
continuous-time LSTM (\nhp) --- free-runs at $66$ events/s against a real rate of
$2.3$, with the Fano factor $6.6\times$ off; its unbounded state-space cousin \sppp{}
reaches $87$ events/s. The field thus faces an \emph{expressivity--stability
frontier}: expressive intensities predict but explode; trivially stable models (an
unconditional LSTM sampler) simulate innocuously but predict poorly.

\ssppp{} is a head transplant, not a new backbone. We keep the stacked
latent-linear-Hawkes state-space backbone of \sppp{} verbatim --- its diagonal
zero-order-hold (ZOH) dynamics, parallel-scan computability, and LayerNorm'd stack
output $u(t)$ --- and replace only what comes after $u(t)$:
\begin{itemize}
\item a \textbf{softmin-bounded rate head}: a gated bounded state
  $h=\sigma(W_o u)\odot\tanh(u)$ is read out through a smooth one-sided cap
  $z = c-\mathrm{softplus}(c-w^{\!\top}h-b)$, so
  $\lambda = s\cdot\mathrm{softplus}(z) \le s\cdot\mathrm{softplus}(c)$ is a
  \emph{hard closed-form ceiling} --- an exact dominating rate for Ogata thinning ---
  while $z\to z_{\text{raw}}$ as $z_{\text{raw}}\to-\infty$, so the floor is exactly
  zero and log-intensity keeps unit gradient in the quiet regime;
\item a \textbf{rate-neutral mark head}: $p^*(k\mid t)=\mathrm{softmax}(\mathrm{MLP}(u))_k$
  lives on the probability simplex, so $\sum_k \lambda_k = \lambda$ holds regardless
  of how nonlinear the mark network is --- expressiveness goes where it cannot
  destabilize the rate.
\end{itemize}

Because the backbone is identical, differences against \sppp{} are attributable to
the heads. The heads, however, are only the first ingredient: we show that the
prediction--simulation gap decomposes into \emph{independent} causes --- what the
model can remember (roll-out memory), what states it trains on (cold-start windows
vs.\ warm streams), and how the likelihood measures intensity mass (the compensator
estimator) --- and we resolve each with a single-factor ablation. Our contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The \ssppp{} head pair} (softmin-bounded rate $\times$ rate-neutral
  marks): a provably bounded, thinning-exact intensity on an unmodified
  state-of-the-art backbone.
\item \textbf{A corrected, identically-configured seven-model benchmark} on Gemini
  ETH-USD order flow (62 event types), covering both per-event prediction (NLL split,
  accuracy, time-rescaling calibration) and closed-loop simulation (stylized-facts
  fit), plus a mechanistic head-ablation chain (two-sided bound $\to$ slow-mode
  initialization $\to$ open-floor softmin) that localizes and cuts the quiet-regime
  deficit by 41\%.
\item \textbf{Carried-state simulation and stateful (TBPTT) training.} Sliding-window
  roll-out architecturally truncates memory to the window span; an $O(1)$-per-event
  carried-state roll-out (exactly equivalent to encoding the full history, for
  state-space recursions) unlocks long-memory volatility clustering. Training with
  state carried across windows (detached at boundaries) improves prediction across
  every metric and brings simulated clustering and return-autocorrelation structure
  to near-real levels --- while leaving the free-running rate unchanged, cleanly
  isolating the rate from the state regime.
\item \textbf{Exact post-hoc rate calibration.} Because the \ssppp{} rate head is
  linear in its learnable scale and the thinning ceiling scales with it, the
  free-running rate can be calibrated by a one-dimensional root-find on short probe
  roll-outs --- certificate-preserving, mark-distribution-preserving, and unavailable
  to coupled-head models. Calibration \emph{improves} every structure fact: the
  apparent structure deficit of over-firing simulators is largely a clock-rate
  artifact.
\item \textbf{A cautionary negative result on unbiased compensators.} The
  Mei--Eisner-style global Monte-Carlo compensator, though verifiably unbiased,
  collapses training under gradient clipping and adaptive optimization: its
  heavy-tailed penalty is systematically trimmed and maximum likelihood learns to
  exploit the sparse audit. Estimator variance, not bias alone, decides whether
  likelihood-level calibration succeeds.
\end{enumerate}
