\begin{abstract}
Neural marked temporal point processes (MTPPs) predict the next limit-order-book (LOB)
event well but \emph{simulate} order flow poorly: in closed-loop roll-out their
learned self-excitation runs away, producing event rates an order of magnitude above
real and grossly distorted clustering statistics. We introduce \ssppp{} (Stable
State-Space Point Process), which keeps the expressive \sppp{} state-space backbone
\emph{verbatim} and replaces only the two output heads: a \emph{softmin-bounded rate
head} whose intensity carries a hard closed-form ceiling (an exact dominating rate for
thinning) while its floor remains exactly zero, and a \emph{rate-neutral} soft-max
mark head that can be arbitrarily deep without perturbing the total rate. Because the
backbone is unchanged, every behavioural difference is attributable to the heads. On a
corrected seven-model benchmark on Gemini ETH-USD order flow (identical training
configuration for all models), \ssppp{} comes within 0.3 nats of the best predictor
(the Neural Hawkes CT-LSTM) while holding the best timing calibration in the table and
beats its unbounded \sppp{} parent by more than 1.8 nats. We then show, through a
chain of single-factor ablations, how to turn the bounded predictor into a
\emph{calibrated simulator}: carried-state (unbounded-memory) roll-out unlocks
long-memory volatility clustering that sliding-window simulation architecturally
truncates; truncated-backpropagation-through-time (TBPTT) training on warm states
improves prediction and brings simulated clustering to near-real levels; and the
factorized rate head admits an \emph{exact, certificate-preserving post-hoc rate
calibration} --- a one-dimensional root-find on its scale --- that no coupled-head
model supports. The full recipe simulates at near-real event rates with
close-to-empirical clustering and return-autocorrelation structure while remaining
provably bounded and exactly samplable. A negative result completes the picture: the
textbook unbiased Monte-Carlo compensator, despite correct expectation, collapses
training under clipped adaptive optimization --- estimator variance, not bias alone,
governs whether likelihood-based calibration succeeds.
\end{abstract}
