\section{Methods}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Event constructor}
\label{subsec:event-construction}
\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.88\columnwidth]{figs/pipeline}
\caption{Pipeline. (1)~\emph{Data collector}: synchronized trade records and
Level-2 order book updates. (2)~\emph{Event constructor}: consecutive book
snapshots are differenced and aligned with trades to emit atomic typed
events. (3)~\emph{Model training}: the event sequence drives the state-space
backbone (LLH layers) whose continuous-time hidden representation $h_t$
decodes the bounded total rate $\lambda(t)$ and the rate-neutral mark
distribution $p(e\mid t)$, giving per-type intensities
$\lambda_e(t)=\lambda(t)\,p(e\mid t)$.}
\label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

We represent market dynamics as a sequence of typed events over the top
$K=10$ price levels on each side of the book (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}).
An \emph{atomic event type} $e=(c,s,\delta)\in\mathcal{E}$ combines an event
class $c$, a side $s\in\{b,a\}$ (bid or ask), and a level index
$\delta\in\{1,\dots,K\}$. The four classes are \textbf{limit order} (LO,
liquidity added at one of the top-$K$ levels), \textbf{cancel order} (CO,
liquidity withdrawn), \textbf{market order} (MO, execution against the best
quote), and \textbf{inside-spread order} (IS, a new order improving the best
quote). For LO/CO, $\delta$ is the distance from the best quote; for IS, the
number of ticks of improvement; for MO, $\delta=1$ always.

Events are constructed by differencing consecutive Level-2 snapshots and
aligning them with trade records at identical timestamps. Because trades and
book updates are synchronized in both time and volume, every observed change
is classified unambiguously by a three-step decision tree
(Figure~\ref{fig:graph_infer}): a liquidity
decrease at the best quote with a matching trade is an MO; a decrease at any
level without one is a CO; an increase inside the spread is an IS; any other
increase is an LO. This maps raw book and trade data exhaustively onto atomic
events.

\subsection{Stable State-Space Point Process (SS2P2)}
\ssppp{} factors the per-type intensity into a bounded total rate and a
rate-neutral mark distribution, the ground-intensity--mark factorization of
set-valued MTPPs \citep{chang2024probabilistic}, both decoded from the
\emph{same} continuous-time hidden representation $h_t$:
\[
\lambda_e(t)\;=\;\lambda(t)\cdot p(e\mid t),
\qquad
\sum\nolimits_{e\in\mathcal{E}} \lambda_e(t)=\lambda(t),
\]
the second identity holding because $\sum_{e} p(e\mid t)=1$.
The design principle is to put nonlinearity where it cannot perturb the rate.

\subsection{Softmin-bounded rate head}
The closed-loop failure of \sppp{} is a feedback loop: each sampled event
raises the intensity, a higher intensity produces events faster, and with an
unbounded head the roll-out drifts hot or explodes. The rate head is built to
break this loop. From the backbone's continuous-time hidden representation
$h_t$ (LayerNorm'd, hence bounded in scale), a gate produces a bounded state
$g$, read out through a smooth one-sided cap with fixed constant $z_{\max}$:
\begin{align}
o &= \sigma(W_o h_t + b_o)\in(0,1)^H,\notag\\
g &= o\odot\tanh(h_t)\in(-1,1)^H,\notag\\
z_{\mathrm{raw}} &= w^{\!\top}g+b \ \ (\text{unconstrained}),\notag\\
z &= z_{\max}-\mathrm{softplus}\bigl(z_{\max}-z_{\mathrm{raw}}\bigr),\notag\\
\lambda(t) &= \varsigma\cdot\mathrm{softplus}(z)\notag
\end{align}
with a learnable positive scale $\varsigma$ initialized so the baseline rate
matches a target rate ($g\!\to\!0 \Rightarrow \lambda \approx
\varsigma\ln 2$). This makes four deliberate design choices:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
\item \textbf{Bounded input.} The readout sees $g\in(-1,1)^H$, not the raw
  latent state: however far the closed loop drives $h_t$, including states
  training never visited, an extreme state can change the direction of $g$
  but not its magnitude.
\item \textbf{Hard ceiling.} Since $\mathrm{softplus}(x)>0$ for every $x$,
  $z\le z_{\max}$ holds identically, so
  $\lambda(t)\le \varsigma\cdot\mathrm{softplus}(z_{\max})$ for every state
  and every parameter setting. No training outcome can remove the bound. The
  worst the roll-out can do is behave as a homogeneous Poisson process at the
  ceiling, and the ceiling doubles as an \emph{exact} dominating rate for
  Ogata thinning.
\item \textbf{Vanishing gain.} Differentiating the cap gives
  $\partial z/\partial z_{\mathrm{raw}} = \sigma(z_{\max}-z_{\mathrm{raw}})$:
  as a burst pushes the intensity toward the ceiling, the head becomes
  exponentially \emph{less} sensitive to further state excursions, so the
  feedback loop loses gain exactly when it is most dangerous. This is what
  makes the closed-loop rate respond smoothly to rescaling, and hence what
  makes the model calibratable.
\item \textbf{Open floor.} The cap is one-sided: as
  $z_{\mathrm{raw}}\to-\infty$, $z\to z_{\mathrm{raw}}$, so
  $\lambda \sim \varsigma\,e^{z_{\mathrm{raw}}}\to 0$ and $\log\lambda$
  keeps unit gradient however quiet the market gets.
\end{itemize}
The first three choices own simulation stability and calibratability; the
fourth ensures the bound costs nothing in prediction when the market is quiet.

\subsection{Rate-neutral mark head}
Marks are decoded from the same $h_t$ but never change the total rate:
\[
p(e\mid t)=\mathrm{softmax}\bigl(\mathrm{MLP}(h_t)\bigr)_e,
\qquad
\lambda_e(t)=\lambda(t)\,p(e\mid t).
\]
Because the soft-max lives on the simplex, the mark network can be arbitrarily
deep (or later conditioned on exogenous state such as an agent's resting
quotes) without touching $\lambda$. This decouples the two failure modes:
rate stability is owned entirely by the bounded head, mark expressiveness
entirely by the simplex.

\subsection{Likelihood and sampling}
Training minimizes the negative log-likelihood of each training window,
covering its events $(t_i,e_i)$ and the next event after the window:
\[
\mathcal{L}
= \int_{0}^{T}\!\lambda(u\mid\mathcal{H}_u)\,du
\;-\;\sum_{i}\Bigl[\log\lambda(t_i) + \log p(e_i\mid t_i)\Bigr],
\]
with $T$ the next-event time. By the factorization, the timing terms train
only the bounded rate head and the mark term (a categorical cross-entropy)
trains only the soft-max head. Each $\lambda(t_i)$ is evaluated at the left
limit, the state just before event $i$, so an event never informs its own
intensity. The compensator integral is approximated by one intensity
evaluation per inter-event interval (the endpoint rule). This under-weights a
decaying intensity and biases the learned rate upward, which the post-hoc
calibration below corrects. Closed-loop sampling uses Ogata thinning with the
exact dominating rate $\varsigma\cdot\mathrm{softplus}(z_{\max})$: propose
arrivals from a homogeneous Poisson process at the ceiling and accept each
with probability $\lambda(t)/\bigl(\varsigma\,\mathrm{softplus}(z_{\max})\bigr)$.

\subsection{Carried-state simulation}
The standard roll-out protocol re-encodes a sliding window of the last $W$
events from a zero state at every step: $O(W)$ work per simulated event, and
a hard memory ceiling, since nothing older than $W$ events can influence the
future and long-memory stylized facts (the slow decay of $|r|$
autocorrelation) become \emph{architecturally} unreachable. Because the
backbone is a state-space recursion, both costs vanish: we carry the packed
decoder state and advance it with only the new event, mathematically
identical to encoding the entire history from the seed, at $O(1)$ cost per
event. Attention- and window-shaped baselines have no such Markov state and
retain the sliding window.

\subsection{Stateful (TBPTT) training}
Windowed maximum likelihood encodes every training window from a cold initial
state, so the model only ever trains in a ``history just began'' transient
and MLE explains the observed rate with an inflated baseline. The free
roll-out, whose state is warm, then double-counts. We train instead with
truncated backpropagation through time: batch lanes walk contiguous stretches
of the stream in order, and the decoder state at the end of window $t$ is
passed, \emph{detached}, as the initial state of window $t{+}1$ (resetting
only at genuine stream boundaries): gradients remain window-local, state
values flow from the start of the stream. This makes training consistent with
carried-state simulation at unchanged per-step cost. It requires
non-overlapping windows (stride $=$ window length), so our controls match
that setting.

\subsection{Post-hoc rate calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}
The factorization buys one more property:
$\lambda=\varsigma\cdot\mathrm{softplus}(z)$ is \emph{exactly linear} in the
learnable scale $\varsigma$, the thinning ceiling scales with it, and the
mark head is untouched by construction. The free-running rate, monotone but
nonlinear in $\varsigma$ through the closed-loop feedback, can therefore be
calibrated by a one-dimensional geometric bisection on a multiplier $\kappa$:
roll out briefly at $\kappa\,\varsigma$, compare the realized rate to the
target, iterate over a handful of probe roll-outs. The result is a
certificate-preserving, sampling-exact simulator at any prescribed mean rate.
For \emph{any} decoder, a constant $\kappa$ multiplying the sim-time
intensity is still mark-preserving (type probabilities $\lambda_e/\lambda$
are unchanged), so the same bisection applies to every baseline. What is lost
off-family is only the exactness of the thinning bound. We make the procedure
falsifiable: the target is the \emph{validation}-split rate, the bisection
must bracket and converge, and a full-scale validation roll-out must verify
the target within tolerance (failures escalate to full-scale probes;
unresolved failures exclude the checkpoint and are reported). Transfer to the
test split is reported, never gated. Calibration corrects the residual rate
inflation from the endpoint-rule compensator and, in the experiments, serves
as a measurement instrument in its own right.
